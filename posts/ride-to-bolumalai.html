Ride to Bolumalai

Posted by Sudharsan on March 5, 2025March 7, 2025

Bolumalai had been on my ride list for some time, as I had been hearing good reviews about the place. When I found out that the BRF (Bangalore Riders Family) team was planning a ride, I couldn’t miss it.

After meeting, greeting, and receiving ride instructions, we started from the Electronic City toll gate around 5:30 AM. We stopped for tea near Sishya School in Hosur, where a casual conversation lightened the mood.

We took the Attibele–Hosur–Sanamavu–Rayakottai–Bolumalai route. Since the highways were under construction with multiple diversions, we opted for the auxiliary road.

Once we crossed Rayakottai, we rode through a few villages. Though narrow, the roads were good. A few of us, following the lead rider Johnson, stopped along a mango plantation and waited for the remaining pack to join. There was no mobile signal in these surroundings.

Once we regrouped, we rode along the plantation for 15 minutes until we reached the diversion to Bolumalai. There was no road—what riders call OFF-ROAD terrain. The next two kilometers consisted of gravel, stones, and dust. The surface was flat yet slippery, but I had a good time riding on it. One has to control speed and balance through the small skids that happen while riding on gravel.

Then came the dirt roads, which were rough and uneven with huge bumps. Many small cave-ins were filled with loose sand, making them even riskier since there was no grip, and the tires slid easily. One could easily lose control unless they navigated through the hard dirt track between the loose sand. When there was no hard track, we had to maintain momentum while riding on the loose sand. Hard braking here was out of the question, as it would almost certainly result in a fall. Even slowing down had to be done gradually. I was excited and mostly rode standing during this bumpy section. I stopped a few times to enjoy the view, took some snaps, and then continued uphill.

Upon reaching the first stop on the mountain, we had to pay an entry fee of ₹20. Minimal snacks and breakfast were available there. From here, we had a cement road for the next 500 meters, but there were no barriers at the edges. However, this section felt fairly easy compared to the dirt road we had just ridden on.

Earlier, vehicles could go all the way to the top, but now many rocks had been broken down, possibly for construction, and were scattered everywhere. One could still try to ride up, but it was riskier and might require assistance while climbing.

We parked and walked to the top, where there was a small temple. However, most people come here for the experience rather than the temple itself. Unfortunately, the place was already littered. A small makeshift shop was selling Maggi and snacks.

The weather was pleasant, so we stayed at the top for almost an hour—clicking pictures and chatting about bikes and rides. Once the sun got hotter, we came down to the small shop and had breakfast, which was okay.

As the heat intensified, we decided to return to Bangalore. We started riding downhill, and I stood up most of the time, using only the first gear and clutch to navigate. In first gear, simply holding the clutch allows the bike to roll downhill, and when it gets too fast, releasing the clutch slows it down naturally. My bike has aggressive engine braking, which helped a lot. I thoroughly enjoyed the downhill ride—I was excited and thrilled.

Once I reached the bottom, I waited for the other riders to join. When the lead rider came down, we rode a little ahead and had to wait for the rest of the pack. We relaxed and refreshed ourselves near the mango plantation and farm fields.

Once everyone regrouped, we rode back to Attibele, had lunch at Attibele Biryani, and then dispersed to our homes.

Ride Date: 2 March 2025
